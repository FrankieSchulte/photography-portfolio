# Photography Portfolio — Research Synthesis and Build Prompt

Status: research and creative direction complete; implementation has not started.

This document is the source of truth for the future website build. It converts the research into a focused strategy for a multidisciplinary photographer who wants to remain available for varied commissions while deliberately moving toward concert and music photography.

## 1. Executive direction

Build a modern photography portfolio that feels like an independent arts magazine crossed with a live-music contact sheet: bold, editorial, image-led, slightly raw, and highly considered.

The site must not present the photographer as someone who simply shoots everything. Its unifying point of view is human energy, identity, and place. Concerts are the leading edge of the brand; portraits, stylized portraits, events, graduation work, and occasional nature/landscape work prove range without weakening that focus.

The experience should make two conclusions immediate:

1. This photographer has a distinctive visual eye and is serious about music and concert work.
2. This photographer is professional, versatile, approachable, and easy to hire for other people-centered commissions.

The website has two jobs: make memorable work easy to experience and turn appropriate visitors into qualified inquiries.

## 2. Research basis

### Live portfolio references

- [Pooneh Ghana](https://www.poonehghana.com/) uses an unapologetic condensed wordmark, vivid color, oversized category typography, and clearly separates live music, music portraits, records, covers, travel, and other formats. The lesson is to make music a world of its own, not one anonymous gallery among many.
- [Danny Clinch](https://www.dannyclinch.com/) opens directly into a black, image-first work wall with almost no explanatory friction. His information page adds high-value credibility through publications, album covers, campaigns, books, awards, and a direct booking path. The lesson is to let the images lead, then substantiate them with concise proof.
- [Christie Goodwin](https://christiegoodwin.com/) uses dramatic negative space, tiny editorial navigation, and a single dominant live image. The lesson is that scale and restraint can make concert work feel like collected art rather than event coverage.
- [Campbell Addy](https://campbelladdy.com/) pairs a dark full-bleed hero with refined serif typography, subtle graphic texture, and distinct routes for selected work, exhibitions, films, books, prints, biography, and contact. The lesson is to create a complete artistic world while keeping every professional offering legible.
- [Jack Davison](https://www.jackdavison.co.uk/) offers both sequence and thumbnail/index ways to browse. The lesson is that a portfolio can support slow, cinematic viewing and fast art-director scanning without duplicating the content.
- [Nadine Ijewere](https://www.nadineijewere.co.uk/) uses a fixed, minimal left rail and gives nearly all remaining space to one photograph. The lesson is that stable, spare navigation can make varied image proportions feel calm and premium.
- [Petra Collins](https://petra-collins.com/) uses a utilitarian left index, monospaced type, and full-height image strips while separating artwork, editorial, commercial, moving image, contact, and CV. The lesson is that an archive-like interface can feel artistic while still distinguishing commercial from personal practice.
- [Ben Hecht](https://benhechtcreative.com/) is a useful counterpoint: a bold serif headline, split-screen image, clear positioning, and obvious calls to action. The lesson is that artistic presentation should not hide what the photographer does or how to inquire.
- [Focus & Framed Photography](https://www.focusandframedphotography.com/) is less visually experimental but useful for its exact music-photography positioning and clear divisions among artists, concerts, publications, festivals, and contact.

### Portfolio and business research

- A portfolio is a curated visual argument, not an archive. Shopify recommends roughly 10–20 excellent images per niche as a starting point, organized into logical categories and sequenced with strong opening and closing images. [Shopify photography portfolio guide](https://www.shopify.com/blog/how-to-make-a-photography-portfolio)
- Ruthless curation, a fast mobile experience, explicit location and accepted work, social proof, and a direct inquiry route are recurring recommendations for portfolios intended to book clients. [Cemhan Biricik portfolio guide](https://cemhanbiricik.com/blog/photography-portfolio-websites.html)
- For event-oriented work, showing two or three short sequences from the same assignment proves consistency more effectively than a wall of unrelated lucky frames. [12img portfolio guidance](https://www.12img.com/blog/photography-portfolio-tips)
- Google recommends crawlable HTML image elements, responsive image sources, descriptive filenames, meaningful contextual alt text, representative social images, and high-quality images that are still optimized for speed. [Google Image SEO best practices](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images)
- Above-the-fold hero images should not be lazy loaded; below-the-fold images should be. Responsive derivatives reduce bandwidth while preserving visual quality. [web.dev image performance guidance](https://web.dev/learn/performance/lazy-load-images-and-iframe-elements)
- The site should meet WCAG 2.2 AA fundamentals: readable contrast, visible keyboard focus, usable touch targets, meaningful sequence, and alternatives for non-text content. [WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/)
- Nonessential animation must respect the visitor’s reduced-motion preference. [W3C animation guidance](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/animation-from-interactions)

## 3. Key strategic conclusions

### Adopt

- Immediate photography in the first viewport.
- A small, persistent navigation system with Concerts elevated to a top-level destination.
- Editorial typography, issue-like numbering, captions, credits, hairline rules, and irregular image rhythms.
- Both an immersive sequence view and a fast index/grid view for the flagship concert work.
- Short project sequences that demonstrate an entire show: performer, crowd, venue, atmosphere, and decisive close detail.
- A concise about story, visible location/travel status, professional headshot, direct email, and a structured inquiry form.
- Real client names, publications, artist names, venues, testimonials, or credentials only when supplied and approved.

### Avoid

- A generic template, rounded card grid, floating pill buttons, glassmorphism, gradient-heavy tech styling, or a dashboard-like “bento” layout.
- A long cinematic preloader before visitors can see work.
- Autoplay audio, background music, or video with sound.
- Excessive parallax, scroll hijacking, an oversized novelty cursor, or animation that competes with the photographs.
- A single mixed gallery containing concerts, graduation, landscapes, and portraits with no explanation.
- Huge galleries, repeated near-duplicates, or multiple images that prove the same skill.
- Fake client logos, publications, reviews, prices, availability, awards, or captions.
- Hiding contact information behind several clicks.
- Treating mobile as a compressed desktop collage.

## 4. Brand and positioning

Working positioning until the photographer supplies final language:

> [PHOTOGRAPHER NAME] is a [CITY/REGION]-based photographer documenting live energy, distinct personalities, and the atmosphere around defining moments. Available for concerts, artist and editorial portraits, events, graduations, and select creative commissions.

Working hero line options for later selection:

- Sound. Presence. The frame between.
- People, stages, and moments in motion.
- Photographs with a pulse.
- Live energy. Lasting image.

Recommended umbrella concept: “Photographs with a pulse.” It naturally covers a crowd at a concert, a stylized portrait, a graduation, and a living landscape without sounding like a list of services.

Use specific professional labels rather than vague creative language near conversion points. For example: “Concert, portrait, and event photographer based in [REGION], available to travel.”

## 5. Information architecture

Use a multi-page structure. Keep the primary navigation deliberately short:

- Work
- Concerts
- About
- Inquire

The photographer’s name/wordmark links home. Work opens an editorial index containing every practice area.

Recommended routes:

- Home — high-impact overview and positioning.
- Work — visual index of all categories.
- Concerts — flagship, most art-directed page.
- Portraits — conventional and stylized/editorial portrait sections on one coherent page, with an option to split later if the body of work grows.
- Events — events plus a clearly labeled graduation/milestones section; split graduation into its own route later if search demand and image volume justify it.
- Field Notes — landscape and nature as a personal practice, preventing it from confusing the commercial offer.
- About — concise biography, working approach, location, travel, experience, headshot, real credentials, and a compact services summary.
- Inquire — direct email, Instagram, location/travel note, project form, and expected response language if the photographer supplies it.

Do not put every category in the desktop header. Concerts earns top-level prominence; the rest live in the Work index and remain reachable in one click.

## 6. Homepage experience

### First viewport

- Use a full-bleed or near-full-bleed concert photograph as a real HTML image, not a CSS background.
- Place a large editorial hero statement over or adjacent to the image with reliable contrast.
- Show the photographer’s name, location, and professional role immediately.
- Include one clear action to view Concerts and one quieter action to inquire.
- Keep the header light and stable. On scroll it may invert between paper and ink themes, but it must remain readable.

### Editorial contents strip

Directly after the hero, include a horizontal or stacked “Contents” index:

- 01 Live / Concerts
- 02 Portraits
- 03 Events & Milestones
- 04 Field Notes

Each entry includes one brief phrase, not marketing filler. On desktop, hovering may reveal a single preview image; on touch, the entry simply navigates.

### Flagship concert feature

- Give Concerts the largest section on the home page.
- Use one dominant image, two supporting images with different scales, a large section title, and a small issue-style label such as “Practice 01 / Live Music.”
- Copy should position the work for artists, venues, promoters, festivals, labels, and publications.
- Link to the full concert story and to the inquiry form with Concert/Tour preselected.

### Range without dilution

Show Portraits, Events & Milestones, and Field Notes as distinct editorial spreads rather than equal generic cards. Portraits can be a diptych; Events can use a contact-sheet strip; Field Notes can use a single quiet landscape and generous negative space.

### Credibility and conversion

- Include a concise about excerpt and an authentic headshot.
- If provided, show a restrained “Selected clients / artists / publications / venues” line and no more than two or three short testimonials.
- End with a bold inquiry banner: “Planning a show, portrait, event, or graduation?” followed by location/travel status and a direct action.

## 7. Flagship Concerts page

This is the highest-priority page and should feel like a complete music-photography publication rather than a thumbnail dump.

### Opening

- Full-viewport concert image with the title “Live / Concerts.”
- Small metadata: artist, venue/festival, city, year, and commissioning client/publication only when supplied and permitted.
- A short positioning paragraph: available for live coverage, tour documentation, festival stories, press/editorial, backstage, and artist portraits. Do not claim capabilities the photographer has not actually demonstrated.

### Story structure

- Lead with the single strongest image.
- Include approximately 18–30 images total once the archive supports it; start with fewer if needed.
- Organize the page as two or three mini stories from complete shows, with five to seven consecutive images per story, plus a few standalone hero frames.
- Across the page, prove range: wide venue context, tight expression, peak action, stage design/light, audience reaction, backstage or transition moments when available, vertical and horizontal orientation, large and small venues, difficult lighting, and quieter emotional frames.
- End with a memorable closer rather than whichever image is last in a folder.

### Browsing modes

- Default mode: immersive vertical editorial sequence with varied scale and intentional whitespace.
- Secondary mode: “Index” or “Contact Sheet” displaying quick thumbnails for art directors and busy clients.
- A lightbox may be used for focused viewing, with previous/next controls, keyboard arrows, Escape to close, visible captions, swipe support, and no swipe-only functionality.
- Preserve native aspect ratios wherever possible. Crop only the true hero image and deliberate preview thumbnails.

### Conversion

End with a specific call to action:

> Booking live coverage, tour documentation, festival work, or artist portraits? Tell me about the artist, venue, dates, usage, and deliverables.

Include a direct link to the inquiry form with the project type preselected.

## 8. Supporting portfolio pages

### Portraits

- Present a coherent spectrum from natural portraiture to stylized/editorial work.
- Use section labels such as “Portraits / Observed” and “Portraits / Constructed” rather than separating by arbitrary demographics.
- Show expression, lighting, location, studio/constructed concepts, full body, tight portrait, individual and group work if available.
- Position the page for artist press, editorial, personal branding, graduation portraits, and creative commissions only where the imagery supports those uses.

### Events & Milestones

- Demonstrate consistency across a full event, not isolated highlights.
- Show establishing context, candid human moments, important details, groups, stage/speakers if available, and a closing atmosphere frame.
- Give graduation work a clearly labeled module with an inquiry action. Keep it polished and celebratory, not visually secondary.
- Avoid publishing private client names or sensitive event details without approval.

### Field Notes

- Treat landscape and nature photography as a quiet personal essay.
- Use slower pacing, more negative space, and concise place/year captions when known.
- Keep this page in Work but out of the primary conversion path unless the photographer later offers prints or assignments in this area.

## 9. Visual system

### Art direction

Mood: independent culture magazine, backstage pass, darkroom contact sheet, gallery catalogue, and modern music press.

The visual system must frame the photography instead of repainting it. Boldness should come from type scale, composition, cropping decisions, pacing, and contrast—not decorative clutter.

### Palette

Use a restrained two-theme system:

- Ink: #0A0A0A
- Paper: #F2EFE7
- Signal accent: #D8FF3E acid chartreuse, used sparingly for active states, numbering, and one or two calls to action
- Muted line on dark: rgba(255,255,255,0.24)
- Muted line on light: rgba(10,10,10,0.22)

If the supplied photography clashes with chartreuse, replace the signal accent with one sampled recurring color from the concert archive. Never use several unrelated accent colors.

### Typography

Use three distinct editorial voices with disciplined roles:

- High-contrast display serif for hero lines and oversized section titles. Preferred direction: Instrument Serif or a similarly expressive editorial serif.
- Condensed grotesk for the wordmark, navigation, category titles, and strong labels. Preferred direction: Barlow Condensed or Archivo Narrow.
- Monospaced face for captions, issue numbers, metadata, image counts, and form helper text. Preferred direction: IBM Plex Mono.

Do not use the serif for paragraphs. Body text remains readable and compact. Avoid extremely thin weights for functional labels.

### Layout

- Use a 12-column desktop grid with generous edge margins and a 4-column mobile logic.
- Favor asymmetry, intentional blank space, full-bleed interruptions, uneven diptychs, and occasional controlled overlaps.
- Use hairline rules, editorial folios, image numbers, and small captions as structural devices.
- Do not wrap every image or section in a card.
- Use a fixed or sticky slim header; do not dedicate a permanent wide sidebar on mobile.
- Let landscape images run wide and portrait images retain vertical presence.
- Avoid masonry that creates a chaotic waterfall. Every sequence should feel edited.

### Texture

If texture is used, keep it almost imperceptible: subtle paper grain, halftone, registration mark, or contact-sheet notation. Do not degrade the photographs or place a noisy overlay across every image.

## 10. Motion and interaction

- Page transitions may use a short editorial wipe or fade, but content must remain available immediately.
- Images can reveal with subtle opacity and vertical movement once; do not animate continuously.
- Hover states may invert type, reveal a single preview image, or expose metadata.
- The cursor remains standard or only minimally customized; never make navigation depend on it.
- No scroll hijacking and no mandatory horizontal-scroll gallery.
- All motion must be removed or simplified when prefers-reduced-motion is enabled.
- Keyboard focus must always be visible against both ink and paper backgrounds.
- Menus, lightboxes, and view toggles must be fully keyboard-operable.

## 11. Mobile behavior

- Treat mobile as a purpose-built editorial edition.
- Use one strong image per viewport or a deliberate two-image stack, never a squeezed desktop collage.
- Reduce hero type size without losing hierarchy; avoid text covering important faces.
- Use a simple full-screen menu with large tap targets.
- Keep Concerts and Inquire visible early in the menu.
- Retain captions and metadata but allow them to wrap cleanly.
- The contact form should use appropriate input types and a one-column layout.
- Lightbox controls must remain reachable with a thumb and must not hide behind browser chrome.

## 12. Content curation rules

- Start each gallery with the best relevant image, not necessarily the most recent.
- Use 10–20 excellent images per supporting category as a starting point.
- Use up to 18–30 images on the flagship Concerts page only when the quality and story structure justify the length.
- Every image must add a new subject, scale, light, emotion, composition, or storytelling function.
- Remove near-duplicates and technically weaker images even if the moment was personally meaningful.
- Never mix low-resolution social downloads with finished portfolio exports.
- Maintain color consistency within each sequence without forcing every category into one grade.
- Get approval before naming artists, clients, private events, or graduates when required.

## 13. Inquiry experience

Show the direct email address and Instagram in addition to a form. The form should ask only for information that helps qualify and plan the commission:

- Name
- Email
- Project type: Concert/Tour; Artist or Editorial Portrait; Event; Graduation; Other
- Preferred date or timeline
- City, venue, or location
- Intended usage or deliverables
- Budget range, optional
- Project details

Use concise labels, not placeholder-only inputs. Provide clear success and error states. Do not subscribe visitors to marketing by default. Do not add a newsletter popup unless the photographer explicitly wants and can maintain one.

## 14. Accessibility, performance, and discoverability

### Accessibility

- Target WCAG 2.2 AA.
- Maintain at least 4.5:1 contrast for normal text and 3:1 for large text.
- Use semantic headings and landmark regions in a logical order.
- Provide a skip link, visible focus, meaningful link labels, and keyboard-operable galleries.
- Important portfolio images receive concise contextual alt text describing the photograph’s meaningful content; repeated decorative previews receive empty alt text.
- Do not encode captions or navigation labels inside images.
- Touch targets should be at least 44 by 44 CSS pixels where practical and never below the WCAG minimum.

### Image delivery

- Use real HTML image elements with explicit width and height.
- Generate responsive AVIF/WebP derivatives with a high-quality JPEG fallback where needed.
- Use source sets and sizes so phones do not download desktop originals.
- Eager-load and prioritize the first meaningful hero image; lazy-load work below the fold.
- Preserve color accuracy with sRGB web exports and test dark concert gradients for banding.
- Do not expose original full-resolution client files.

### Search and sharing

- Use a unique title and description per route.
- Working home title: “[PHOTOGRAPHER NAME] — Concert, Portrait & Event Photographer in [REGION].”
- Working concert title: “Concert & Live Music Photography — [PHOTOGRAPHER NAME].”
- Use descriptive image filenames and contextual captions.
- Add canonical URLs, sitemap, robots rules, Open Graph/Twitter metadata, and a bespoke social image that reflects the finished design.
- Add appropriate Organization or ProfessionalService structured data only after real business name, region, email, URL, and service area are known. Do not publish a home address unless the photographer wants it public.
- If organic search is important, create useful evergreen project or service copy around real locations and specialties rather than keyword-stuffed captions.

## 15. Real content required before final production

The workspace currently contains no portfolio assets. Before the design can become a truthful finished site, collect:

- Photographer’s professional name or studio name.
- City/region, travel availability, and service area.
- Professional email, Instagram, and any other approved links.
- Short and long biography; years of experience; working approach.
- One authentic headshot or behind-the-scenes portrait.
- Concert selections, ideally organized by artist/show with artist, venue, city, year, and usage permissions.
- Portrait and stylized-portrait selections.
- Event and graduation selections with privacy approval.
- Landscape/nature selections with location/year where desired.
- Real client, artist, venue, publication, brand, award, or testimonial details.
- Preferred services, availability language, and whether starting prices should be public.
- Copyright year and any image-use notice.

If some categories do not yet have enough excellent work, launch them with fewer images or omit them temporarily. Never fabricate the missing proof.

## 16. Acceptance criteria for the eventual build

The site is ready only when:

- A first-time visitor can identify the photographer’s name, location, specialty, and inquiry path within the first viewport.
- Concerts is clearly the flagship discipline without implying that other commissions are unwelcome.
- Every requested genre has a coherent home and no page feels like miscellaneous leftovers.
- The Concerts page works both as an immersive story and a fast professional index.
- The design feels like a specific arts publication, not a portfolio template or software landing page.
- Desktop and mobile both preserve image impact, caption clarity, and fast access to contact.
- All interactive elements are keyboard-operable and reduced-motion behavior works.
- Hero images load promptly, below-the-fold images load progressively, and layout does not jump while images appear.
- There is no invented client, caption, testimonial, metric, credential, price, or contact detail.
- Metadata, social preview, page titles, image naming, and sitemap are production-ready.
- The inquiry form has been tested end-to-end and direct email remains available as a fallback.

## 17. Final implementation prompt

Build a multi-page photography portfolio for [PHOTOGRAPHER NAME], a [CITY/REGION]-based photographer with several years of experience across concerts, events, graduations, portraits, stylized portraits, and occasional landscape/nature work. The business must remain general enough for varied commissions while establishing concert and music photography as the unmistakable future-facing specialty.

Create an original visual identity described as “independent arts magazine meets live-music contact sheet.” Use an ink-and-paper palette, one restrained signal accent, a high-contrast editorial display serif, a condensed grotesk for the masthead and navigation, and a mono face for captions and image metadata. Use a 12-column asymmetric grid, generous negative space, hairline rules, issue-like numbering, carefully varied image scale, and occasional controlled overlap. Avoid generic card grids, pill-heavy interfaces, glass effects, gradient tech styling, autoplay audio, long preloaders, excessive parallax, scroll hijacking, or decorative effects that compete with the photographs.

Use primary navigation for Work, Concerts, About, and Inquire, with the name linking home. Create routes for Home, Work, Concerts, Portraits, Events, Field Notes, About, and Inquire. Work is the category index. Concerts remains top-level. Portraits combines natural and stylized/editorial sections. Events includes a clear graduation/milestones module. Field Notes holds landscape and nature work as a quieter personal practice.

Open the home page with a real, optimized concert image and immediate positioning: name, region, concert/portrait/event role, one action to view Concerts, and one quieter inquiry action. Follow with an editorial contents index, a large Concerts feature, distinct visual spreads for Portraits, Events & Milestones, and Field Notes, a concise biography with an authentic headshot, real credibility details only when provided, and a final inquiry banner.

Make the Concerts page the most art-directed and complete experience. Lead with the strongest image and concise availability for live coverage, tour documentation, festivals, press/editorial, backstage, and artist portraits only where supported. Sequence approximately 18–30 excellent images as two or three complete show stories plus standalone hero frames, proving venue context, peak action, emotion, stage light, crowd energy, and different scales. Offer an immersive vertical story view and a fast Index/Contact Sheet view. Provide an accessible captioned lightbox with keyboard, close, previous/next, and touch support. End with a concert-specific inquiry action.

Keep supporting galleries rigorously curated at roughly 10–20 excellent images each. Start and end strong. Preserve native aspect ratios, remove near-duplicates, and use captions only where they add useful context. Use real names and credentials only when approved.

Create a professional inquiry page with direct email, Instagram, location/travel status, and a concise accessible form for name, email, project type, date/timeline, city/venue/location, usage/deliverables, optional budget, and details. Never fake form success, credentials, reviews, clients, or prices.

Implement the portfolio as an image-performance product: crawlable image elements, responsive source sets, modern formats with fallbacks, explicit dimensions, prioritized hero image, lazy loading below the fold, descriptive filenames, and safe web-resolution exports rather than original full-resolution files. Add route-specific titles and descriptions, canonical URLs, sitemap, social metadata with a bespoke preview image, meaningful alt text, and appropriate business structured data once real details are known.

Meet WCAG 2.2 AA fundamentals. Use semantic structure, a skip link, visible focus, sufficient contrast, accessible menus/forms/lightboxes, meaningful labels, large touch targets, and a reduced-motion version of every nonessential transition. Design mobile as its own single-column editorial edition, not a shrunken desktop collage.

Use the real supplied photography as the art direction’s source of truth. If a requested category lacks enough strong work, launch with fewer images or omit it temporarily instead of filling the site with placeholders or invented proof. The final result should feel bold and singular, but it must remain fast, legible, easy to navigate, and easy to hire from.
